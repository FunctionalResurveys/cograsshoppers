Key to Juvenile Grasshoppers

Melanoplus differentalis (Thomas)

Illustration by Ali Moore

 




Photos by Gerald M. Fauske

Melanoplus differentalis (the differential grasshopper) ranges widely throughout in North America and Mexico. This is a large grasshopper that is closely related to Melanoplus bivittatus. It is a mid to late season species that commonly feeds on forbs and grasses.

Distribution in Colorado: 1,800 m and lower (6,000 ft and lower)

Hatch Date: Intermediate group, late spring

Body and head color vary between brown and tan with a molted/spotted appearance. The sides of the pronotum are marked with a horizontal white stripe and often the stripe is bordered by black spots/speckles. This becomes less visible in later instars.

The compound eye is brown with a stripe running across half of its length.

A black post ocular band is commonly displayed.

The hind femur face is considered to have a black stripe however, as instars progress chevrons begin to appear, particularly at the proximal end.

The hind tibia is pale gray/green/tan.

 

For more information see the M. differentalis Species Fact Sheet.

 

 

 

Commonly Confused With:

1) M. femerrubrum – M. femerrubrum has much less black on the head whereas, M. dawsoni almost has a completely black head. M. dawsoni has a completely black eye and M. femerrubrum has a two-toned eye, but both lack a stripe. M. femerrubrum hatches later in the season and occurs at lower elevations. Both have yellow undersides. M. dawsoni often show distinctly white lateral regions while M. femurrubrum do not.

2) M. keeleri – M. keeleri has a considerably larger portion of white/cream/yellow on the lower half of the head and pronotum than M. dawsoni. M. keeleri has a distinctive double yellow stripe on the eye, it hatches later and is found at lower elevations than M. dawsoni.
