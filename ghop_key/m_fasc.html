Key to Juvenile Grasshoppers

Melanoplus fasciatus (Walker)

Illustration by Ali Moore

 




Melanoplus fasciatus (the Huckleberry grasshopper) is a largely a montane species with wings of variable length. Moderately common, it can be found near sub-alpine clearings to the lower foothills. This species feeds on huckleberry or blueberry bushes as well as other broad leaf plants.

Distribution in Colorado: 2,000 - 3,300 m (6,700 – 11,000 Ft)

Hatch Date: Intermediate group, hatching in late-May at lower sites to mid-July at the higher sites.

Body and head color vary between brown, green, and tan. The sides of the pronotum are marked with a thin horizontal white stripe extending through the thorax. Above the white is a thick black post ocular band also extending onto the thorax.

The compound eye is tan/brown with the top half being lighter in color than the bottom half. The eye lacks a stripe.

A black post ocular band is commonly displayed.

The hind femur face has two common appearances; a) the stripe can be solid black/brown with a notch at the proximal end or b) the stripe can be broken, black/brown and with a notch at the proximal end.

The hind tibia is normally the same general color as the body color in this species.

For more information see M. fasciatus the Biology of Common Colorado Grasshoppers.

 

 

Commonly Confused With:

1) M. confusus – M. confusus has a solid stripe all the way across the eye and M. fasciatus has a two-toned eye with no stripe. M. confusus hatches before M. fasciatus and appears at lower elevations than M. fasciatus.

 

2) M. sanguinipes – M. sanguinipes has a stripe half way across the eye whereas, M. fasciatus displays a two-toned eye. M. sanguinipes males have a “M” shaped notch on the subgeniteal plate and M. fasciatus does not. M. sanguinipes appears slightly before M. fasciatus though they do overlap in the middle of their ranges. The femur stripe on M. faciatus also has a distinct proximal notch that M. sanguinipes does not. Finally, M. sanguinipes juveniles tend to be tan while M. fasciatus can often be green.
