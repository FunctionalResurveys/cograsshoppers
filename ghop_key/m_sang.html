Key to Juvenile Grasshoppers

Melanoplus sanguinipes (Fabricius)

Illustration by Ali Moore

 



Photo by Gerald M. Fauske

Melanoplus sanguinipes (the migratory grasshopper) is a extremely common inhabitant of grasslands and meadows throughout most of North America. It can reach high densities and as such is an important crop pest. Commonly found throughout the plains and mountains of Colorado.

Distribution in Colorado: <1,500 -3,050 m (<5,000 – 10,000 Ft)

Hatch Date: Early to intermediate group, begin hatching in mid-May and continue to July (perhaps having multiple generations at lower elevations)

Body and head color vary between brown and tan (rarely light green or gray) . The sides of the pronotum are marked with a faint horizontal white stripe, which may not be present in all specimens. Bordering the white stripe are black spots/speckles which are more prominent above the stripe.

The compound eye is brown with a dark stripe crossing half of it. Often there are two white stripes bordering this half stripe.

A black post ocular band is commonly displayed.

The hind femur face has a black/brown broken stripe with a large notch at the proximal end.

The hind tibia can be pale blue/green with a reddish basal ring.

 

For more information see M. sanguinipes Species Fact Sheet.

 

 

Commonly Confused With:

1) M. confusus – M. confusus has a full stripe on the eye whereas, M. sanguinipes only has a half of a stripe. M. confusus males have no “M” shaped notch on the subgeniteal plate like M. sanguinipes. M. sanguinipes occupy slightly higher elevations and hatch later in the season. These two species can be difficult to tell apart.

2) M. fasciatus  M. fasciatus has a two-toned eye with no stripe and males do not have a notch on the subgeniteal plate. Both occur at the same elevation ranges and have similar hatch dates. M. fasciatus males have no M shaped notch on the subgeniteal plate like M. sanguinipes.

3) M. lakinus – M. lakinus and M. sanguinipes may occur in similar lowland areas. M. lakinus do not have distinct femur stripes, rather they may not have a stripe at all or the stripe may be light gray or composed of 2 to 3 gray areas. The eye stripes of M. lakinus are much more evident and dramatic than M. sanguinipes. M. lakinus males have no “M” shaped notch on the subgeniteal plate like M. sanguinipes. Finally, the body color of M. lakinus in the area tends to be light green, while the body color of M. sanguinipes tends to be tan.
