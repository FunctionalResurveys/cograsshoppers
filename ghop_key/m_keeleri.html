Key to Juvenile Grasshoppers

Melanoplus keeleri (Thomas)

Illustration by Ali Moore

 




Photo by Gerald M. Fauske

M. Keeleri (the Keeler grasshopper) ranges widely in North America inhabiting upland grasslands from the Atlantic coast to the Great Basin and from southern Canada to southern United States. In the west it is a common inhabitant of the tallgrass prairie and the taller types of the mixed grass prairie.

Distribution in Colorado: 1,500 – 2,100 m (5,000 – 7000 Ft)

Hatch Date: Late group, early summer in June

Body and head have distinct black regions offset by white/yellow regions. The front of the head is black. The lower distal portion of the head and the lateral portion of the pronotum are largely white with the exception of a small black patch seen in early instars. There is also a distinct yellow ring around the base of the antennae and the underside is bright yellow.

The compound eye is black with two yellow stripes crossing the entire eye.

A post ocular band is absent on this species.

The hind femur face has a solid black stripe.

The hind tibia is normally the same general color as the body color in this species.

 

For more information see M. keeleri Species Fact Sheet.

 

 

Commonly Confused With:

1) M. dawsoni – M. dawsoni does not have the white patch on the lower part of the face and pronotum. M. dawsoni also have solid black eyes whereas, M. keeleri have a double yellow stripe. M. dawsoni is found at slightly higher elevation and hatches earlier than M. keeleri.

2) M. femerrubrum - M. femerrubrum has less black on its head, lacks the white patch on the lower face and pronotum, as well as, the double yellow eye stripe that M. keeleri displays. M. femerrubrum and M. keeleri occur at the same elevations, though M. femerrubrum hatches slightly earlier.
