Key to Juvenile Grasshoppers

Melanoplus packardii (Scudder)

Illustration by Ali Moore

 




Photo by Gerald M. Fauske

Melanoplus packardii (the Packard grasshopper) is a common and abundant species that is primarily found in montane and lowland grasslands and desert prairies. The species also lives in rural habitats and in Colorado it is an important pest species.

Distribution in Colorado: 1,500 – 2,500 m (5,000 – 8,5000 Ft)

Hatch Date: Early to intermediate group, begin hatching in May and continue to August (perhaps having multiple generations at lower elevations)

Body and head color vary between green, tan, and yellow. Generally this species is a very solid color with no distinctive markings.

The compound eye is dark brown/reddish throughout. It lacks a stripe.

A post ocular band is absent on this species.

The hind femur face has two common appearances; a) there may not be a stripe at all or b) there may be black spots/speckles that thicken toward the proximal end.

The hind tibia is pale grey/green/pink.

 

For more information see M. packardii Species Fact Sheet.

 

 

Commonly Confused With:

M. lakinus – M. packardii does not display a crescent on the sides of the pronotum as M. lakinus does. M. packardii has many black spots/speckles on the femur face whereas M. lakinus may have a plain femur face or display 2-3 very faint grey bars. M. packardii lacks an eye stripe whereas M. lakinus has multiple eye stripes. M. packardii hatch earlier and can be found at much higher elevations.
