Gordon Alexander Biography

The biography is excerpted from: Nufio, CR et al. 2009. Gordon Alexander, Grasshoppers, and Climate Change. American Entomologist 55.1: 10.

Gordon Alexander (1901-1973) was a professor at the University of Colorado from 1931 to 1966, where he served as head of the Biology Department for 20 years, from 1939 to 1958. For a brief period during World War II, Alexander also served as acting head of the University of Colorado Museum of Natural History.

Alexander was a distinguished ornithologist and orthopterist whose research produced publications ranging from keys to the grasshoppers of Colorado (Alexander 1941), to descriptions of general adaptations used by organisms living along altitudinal gradients (Alexander 1962; Alexander and Hilliard 1969), to articles on the biogeography of birds (Alexander 1937, 1964a) and the grasshoppers of the Front Range and Rocky Mountain regions of Colorado (Alexander 1964b).During his tenure at the university, Alexander mentored 36 master’s students and 10 doctoral students.

The History of an Avid Naturalist

Edward Gordon Alexander was born on 18 August 1901 in Rich Hill, Missouri, a small rural town on the western border with Kansas.

Gordon's father was a Methodist minister whose vocation required him to move to a new church every four years. The family, which included Gordon and his younger brother, Hendrix, lived in a variety of towns and cities along the Missouri River Valley, including Kansas City, Kansas, and Lexington, Missouri.

Although moving so often would be hard on a young family, Gordon made the most of the situation because it afforded him plenty of opportunity to explore the rural Missouri River Valley. This early exploration led to his fascination with the natural history and biology that surrounded him.

In his youth, Gordon was an avid birdwatcher who would often take his lunch, climb a tree, and sit for hours waiting for birds to come to him. At other times, he would tramp across the floodplains of the Missouri River to observe migrating birds and their feeding habits.

The Grasshopper Survey

During the 1958-1960 field seasons, Gordon and his colleague, John Hilliard, Jr., led a National Science Foundation (NSF)-sponsored survey project to examine the distribution and phenology of Orthoptera species along an elevational gradient in the Rocky Mountains of Colorado (Fig. 3).

The eastern portion of this transect began in the Front Range near Boulder and extended west across a vertical range of more than 3,000 m, from 1,530 m on the plains to 3,660 m above sea level in the western alpine region.

Although their main study focused on surveying 14 sites on a near-weekly basis, they ultimately surveyed more than 300 independent sites and processed almost 65,000 grasshoppers.

By noting the presence of juveniles, they determined which species were residents at a particular site and which were accidentals, meaning species that either flew or were blown into the area (Fig. 4).

This 1958-1960 survey was later published in Ecological Monographs (Alexander and Hilliard 1969), documenting the grasshopper species present along the elevational gradient.

Legacy

On July 31, 1973, Gordon and Marion were tragically killed when the airplane in which they were traveling crashed while landing in Boston. Alexander's extensive Orthoptera research remained in boxes at the CU Museum of Natural History until it was later curated by César Nufio and colleagues.

The Gordon Alexander Orthoptera Collection at the University of Colorado Museum of Natural History is an exceptional half-century-old collection of specimens and field data that provides invaluable historical information about the grasshopper species living near the Rocky Mountains of Boulder.

Gordon Alexander and John Hilliard's 1958-1960 survey provides not only 14,000 vouchered specimens, but also field notebooks containing information on an additional 51,000 specimens processed during their study. Another unique and important contribution of their work is that four of their primary collecting sites were adjacent to permanent weather stations established by John Marr in the early 1950s. Taken together, the 1958-1960 study provides a detailed picture of grasshopper species occurrence, abundance, and phenology along an elevational gradient in the Rocky Mountains of Colorado 50 years ago.
